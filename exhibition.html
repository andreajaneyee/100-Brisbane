Country

Members of the local Aboriginal community share their thoughts on what Country means to them.

The modern history of Brisbane began with the arrival of Europeans in 1823, however for the Aboriginal people of the region this area has a continuous and ongoing history going back tens of thousands of years. 3 June 1992 marks the date when the High Court of Australia ruled that the lands of Australia were not 'terra nullius' when European settlement occurred. Then the Native Title Act 1993 formally recognised Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people's claim to Country. The struggle for land rights continues and for the recognition of Aboriginal sovereignty. Country is complex, being both spiritual and practical. It describes the environment, but also the past; it binds Aboriginal people to the place of their ancestors, imbued with stories and responsibilities. Country has always been and continues to be sacred to, and respected by Aboriginal people.

"The vision of my Country, the way I view or see my Country, the way I talk or sing up my Country, the way I talk of the stories of my Country and talk about my Elders and Ancestors, this is my cultural heritage. The way I call their names as if walk the sacred places of my Country and the way I remember their brave deeds on my kind is my cultural heritage. My spiritual connection with my land is my cultural heritage. All these things are a part of my cultural heritage.

To walk my Country, to gather the shell from the ocean beaches and the bay side beaches is part of my cultural heritage. To see the changing nature of the flora with the seasons, to gather the wild flowers and to eat the berries and fruits of mg Country reminds me of my mother and her connection to this my cultural heritage. To observe the birds, their migratory flight Patterns, and nesting habitats, to understand how their presence fertilises, pollinates and regenerates the plant growth, how they herald the arrival of the deep-sea mullet, the whales and other sap inhabitants for seasonal sustenance. This is the cultural heritage of my Country.

To be with my family and community people walking the Country together, making that strong spiritual connection with the land is my cultural heritage. To continue to walk the bora ground and practice my cultural rights and responsibilities, as well as acknowledge the importance of this continuation on my Country is my cultural heritages.

If we do not have access to our land we are denied the right to maintain our practices that protect, preserve and nurture our land and our cultural heritage."

Uncle Bob Anderson
Gheebelum, Ngugi, Mulgumpin

Meanjin to Brisbane

As Brisbane developed, thousands of Aboriginal people were dispossessed of their land.

The Moreton Bay penal settlement was established on a ridge on the north side of the Brisbane River, an area known as Meanjin, in early 1825. It is estimated that there were approximately 5,000 Aboriginal people living between Pine River in the north to Logan River in the south.

The Commandants of the penal settlement were under strict orders on how to interact with the local Aboriginal people. Good relations were generally fostered and maintained between the penal settlement and the many Aboriginal groups. As the planting of the settlement's crops on the southern side of the river increased and the presence of the new European arrivals became more permanent, there was an increase in tensions and raids on the crops became more regular. This resulted in a greater presence of armed guards and, inevitably, conflict.

Tensions further increased after Brisbane was opened to free settlement in May 1842. The free settlers were not governed by military orders. Many held the social views of the time and, consequently, did not treat the Aboriginal people with common decency. In response, the various Aboriginal groups shifted away from their more accommodating stance to one of retaliation as they tried to enforce their own system of law on the newcomers.

Escalating moments of frontier violence by settlers, retaliatory attacks by Aboriginal warriors and the implementation of British law resulted in the imprisonment and deaths of many senior Aboriginal men, eroding the leadership of the Aboriginal resistance. Even though the struggle for rights and recognition never stopped, the introduction of a curfew by 1852, where all Aboriginal people in Brisbane had to leave the town by 4pm, represented a significant moment in the loss of connection between the local Aboriginal people and their Country.
